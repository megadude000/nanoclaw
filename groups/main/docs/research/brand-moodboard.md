## 1. DTC Coffee Brand Analysis — 15 References

### 1.1 Onyx Coffee Lab (USA)
- **Colors**: Deep charcoal `#1A1A1A`, cream `#F5F0E8`, deep burgundy accent `#5C1A1A`
- **Typography**: Serif display (custom Onyx wordmark) + clean sans body text
- **Packaging**: Matte black bags with foil deboss; heavy stock; jewellery-box unboxing feel
- **Website vibe**: Editorial luxury magazine meets science journal; long-form origin content
- **What works**: The "fine dining" positioning at coffee price point — makes you feel like you're buying art. High photography standards set a benchmark.
- **Takeaway for YourWave**: Serif + editorial layout signals "we take this seriously"

### 1.2 Intelligentsia Coffee (USA)

- **Packaging**: Striking illustrated bags — each origin gets a distinct illustration
- **Website vibe**: Brand heritage + social justice narrative; dense content but well-organized
- **What works**: Illustration as differentiator — each bag is collectible art; strong community story
- **Takeaway for YourWave**: Origin-specific illustration could turn Atlas articles into visual identity

### 1.3 Blue Bottle Coffee (USA/Japan)

- **Website vibe**: Zen minimalism; Japanese influence; whitespace as luxury

## 2. Patterns & Trends Across Successful Brands

### 2.1 What the Best DTC Coffee Brands Share

**Restraint in color palette**
- Almost every successful brand uses 2–3 colors maximum
- Black/white/one accent is most common (Onyx, Blue Bottle, The Barn)
- Warm neutrals (cream, kraft, sand) consistently outperform cool greys in premium positioning

**Typography hierarchy is everything**
- Strong brands use 2 typefaces maximum: one display/headline, one body
- Serif for heritage/trust; geometric sans for modern/precision; humanist sans for approachable
- Handwritten or custom lettering signals artisan authenticity (Oatly, Heart, Stumptown)

**Photography over illustration (mostly)**
- Origin photography with real people and places is the dominant trend
- Illustration used as differentiator (Intelligentsia, Five Elephant) — works when executed consistently
- Flat lays with props: very 2018; declining in credibility in 2026

**Story in every touchpoint**
- Every successful brand tells a story — origin, founder, mission, process
- The story is embedded in packaging copy, website headers, social captions
- Czech market is underserved on this — almost no Czech coffee brand tells a compelling narrative

**Packaging as the first impression**
- The bag/box is the product for DTC coffee
- Matte finishes outperform glossy in premium positioning
- Functional details (valve placement, resealable closure) matter to specialty coffee buyers

## 3. Anti-Patterns to Avoid

### Visual Anti-Patterns

| Anti-Pattern | Why It Fails | Better Alternative |
|-------------|--------------|-------------------|
| Generic coffee beans as hero image | Zero differentiation; every commodity brand does this | Landscape, farm, or process photography |
| Brown gradient backgrounds | Looks like mass-market commodity; Czech generic brands | Neutral cream, sage, or dark editorial |
| Too many fonts (3+) | Reads as amateur; dilutes brand identity | 2 fonts max: display + body |
| Gradient logos | Looks like 2014 startup; dates poorly | Single-color, vector-first logo design |
| Script fonts for "artisan" cue | Overused; no longer signals craft | Handwritten from actual founder; custom lettering |
| Glossy packaging | Feels cheap vs. competition in specialty segment | Matte with spot varnish for accents |
| Coffee cup as primary brand image | Lazy visual shorthand | Origin, process, or abstract brand mark |
| Rainbow color palette | Scattered, unfocused; no one does it well in premium | Strict 2–3 color palette |

### Strategic Anti-Patterns

| Anti-Pattern | Why It Fails |
|-------------|--------------|
| Trying to look American | Czech/European aesthetic is differentiator, not liability |
| Avoiding Czech language in brand | Alienates primary market; feels imported not local |
| Generic sustainability claims | Every brand says this now; specifics > generics |
| Price competing on marketplaces | Destroys premium positioning before it's established |
| Over-explaining specialty coffee | Comes across as condescending; lead with experience |

## 4. Color Palette Recommendations for YourWave

YourWave's existing sage/amber palette is already strong. Refinement recommendations:

### Primary Palette

| Role | Color | Hex | Rationale |
|------|-------|-----|-----------|
| Primary | Sage Green | `#7A8E72` | Grounding; nature; origin; calm authority |
| Primary | Warm Amber | `#C4862A` | Warmth; harvest; premium; wave energy |
| Base | Off-white | `#F5F0E6` | Cream not pure white; feels analog, handcrafted |
| Dark | Charcoal | `#2A2A2A` | Not pure black; softer; sophisticated |

### Secondary Palette (accents, photography filters, seasonal)

| Role | Color | Hex | Rationale |
|------|-------|-----|-----------|
| Cool accent | Stone Blue | `#6B8CA8` | Water/wave; coffee origin elevation; pairs with amber |
| Warm neutral | Sand | `#D4C4A0` | Background gradient; transitions between sage and amber |
| Deep contrast | Forest | `#3D5A44` | Darker sage for depth in compositions |

### Palette Usage Rules
- **Digital (website/Instagram)**: Sage primary + amber accent; off-white backgrounds; charcoal text
- **Packaging**: Matte sage base with amber typography; off-white labels
- **Photography filter**: Warm golden tones; slight desaturation; film-like grain; never oversaturated
- **Dark mode**: Charcoal background + sage accent + amber highlight

### What to Avoid
- Pure #FFFFFF (too cold for brand warmth)
- Pure #000000 (too harsh; use charcoal)
- Bright saturated greens (too energetic; undermines calm authority)
- Red or pink accents (inconsistent with sage/amber warmth)

## 5. Typography Recommendations

### Display / Headline Typeface
**Recommendation**: A humanist serif with personality — something like:
- **Freight Display** (editorial, warm, trustworthy) — used by editorial brands
- **Canela** (contemporary serif; feels both modern and rooted) — premium positioning
- **Libre Baskerville** (free; open source; classic but not stuffy) — good starting point

**Why serif for headlines**: Signals permanence, authority, craft — exactly what specialty coffee needs to communicate. Czech design heritage (think Czech book covers, posters) is serif-literate.

### Body / UI Typeface
**Recommendation**: Clean humanist sans-serif:
- **Inter** (free; purpose-built for screens; excellent readability)
- **DM Sans** (free; slightly warmer than Inter; good for brand voice)
- **Nunito** (rounder; more approachable; good if brand voice leans warm)

### Type Scale Principles
- Headline: 32–48px / display uses tracking +0.02–0.04em
- Subhead: 20–24px / medium weight
- Body: 16–18px / regular weight / 1.6–1.8 line height
- Caption: 12–14px / light weight / all-caps with +0.08em tracking

### Czech Language Typography Notes
- Czech has diacritical marks (á, č, ě, š, ž, ů, ř, etc.) — verify font supports all characters
- Inter, DM Sans, and Freight all have excellent Czech character support
- Avoid fonts that display Czech diacritics as visual afterthoughts (common in decorative fonts)

## 6. Photography Style Guide

### Visual Direction: "Golden Hour Atlas"
Warm, unhurried, documentary — the visual language of a slow morning with extraordinary coffee.

### Style Principles

**Color treatment**
- Warm golden tones; amber-biased color grading
- Slight desaturation in shadows; let highlights stay warm
- Film grain texture overlay (subtle; 10–15% opacity) for analog feel
- Avoid cool blue tones; avoid oversaturated greens

**Lighting**
- Golden hour / soft window light — the only light that works for YourWave's aesthetic
- Hard shadows occasionally acceptable for dramatic product shots
- Flash or artificial light: only for behind-the-scenes / process shots

**Composition**
- Negative space is generosity — don't fill every corner
- Rule of thirds loosely applied; often centered for calm authority
- Handmade imperfection preferred over sterile perfection

**Subjects**
- Hero: Coffee in brewing environment (V60 drip, steam rising, pour in motion)
- People: Hands interacting with coffee; faces occasionally but not stock-photo cheerful
- Origin: Real places — farm landscapes, market scenes, drying beds
- Product: Bag on natural surface (linen, wood, stone); never white seamless

**What to avoid**
- Coffee art latte art close-ups (it's a filter coffee brand first)
- Posed, smiling people holding cups (feels inauthentic)
- Stock photography of any kind
- Studio white backgrounds (exception: packaging product shots only)
- Over-filtered Instagram coffee aesthetic (orange + teal LUTs are dated)

### Shot List for Core Asset Library
1. V60 pour sequence (3–4 frame series)
2. Bag of coffee on stone/wood surface, natural light
3. Cupping scene (multiple cups, spoons, notes)
4. Raw coffee cherries or green beans (origin)
5. Morning window light with coffee and atlas/book
6. Founder hands at work (grinding, pouring)
7. Czech café exterior (collab location)
8. Map or atlas page with coffee marked
